The caravan creaked like it remembered better days. Its wheels dragged along
the dusty road, whispering stories of everywhere it had been and nowhere it had
stayed. Paint peeled from its sides in thin curls, like it was slowly shedding
an old identity it could no longer carry. Still, it moved.

Inside, sunlight slipped through thin curtains, painting soft gold lines across
worn cushions and chipped mugs that rattled gently with every bump in the road.
There was a quiet rhythm to it—the hum of movement, the soft clatter of
forgotten things, the occasional sigh of the wind pressing against its fragile
frame. It felt alive in a way that houses never did.

Whoever traveled with it had learned not to collect too much. Space was
limited, and so were attachments. A folded map tucked into the glove
compartment. A sweater draped over the back of a seat. A photograph, slightly
bent at the corners, of a place that might have once been called home.

But the caravan didn’t believe in staying.

Every mile carried a quiet promise: that something new waited just beyond the
next bend, even if it looked a lot like yesterday. The same empty roads. The
same sky stretching endlessly above. The same questions with no real answers.

And yet, there was comfort in that repetition. In knowing that movement itself
was a kind of purpose. That even without a destination, the journey meant
something.

As evening fell, the caravan slowed. The engine softened into silence, and the
world grew still around it. Somewhere far off, a light flickered on. The
curtains swayed slightly, catching the last of the day.

For a moment, it almost felt like stopping.

But morning would come.
And the road would still be there.
